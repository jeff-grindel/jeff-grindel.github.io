Sous Vide

Sous-Vide is a method of cooking food in a sealed plastic bag in a water bath for a longer period of time. The water bath needs to be at a constant temperature (around 125 °F to 140 °F) for a long period of time in order to cook the food evenly. Wikipedia

Instead of buying one off the shelf, I thought it would be a good idea to build one from scratch using my Arduino Microcontroller.

Setup/Wiring:

The temperature probe did not come ready to be plugged into the breadboard directly out of the box. I simply striped the wires back and soldered the from the temperature probe to a single core wire. This allowed me to plug the temperature probe directly into the breadboard. (Red: +Vdd, White/Green: Data, Black: GND)

The LCD screen also needed to be soldered to the male/male header that was included in box. Pictures seen below.



To wire this circuit I am using 8 of the digital pins on the Arduino MicroController. 6 of those being used to control the LCD, 1 used to as the Data input for the temperature probe, and 1 used to control the AC power switch. Below are the connection tables for my set-up. On the Power Switch Tail II, the Ground connection (pin 3) is connected internally to the AC side electrical safety ground (the green conductor) and can be used if needed. Source

Final Assembly:

After loading the code on the Arduino Microcontroller and wiring everything up according to the schematic, the room temperature was able to be displayed on the LCD.

A simple way to test the switching of the Power Tall 2 was to simply set the temperature threshold in the code to a low number (I used around 80) and heat up the temperature probe by rubbing it and seeing if the red LED on the module turned off. This would indicate that there was no AC power flowing through the PowerSwitch. Once satisfied with everything, it was time to hook everything up and start cooking.

Cooking:

Instead of a crockpot I used a simple water boiler to heat the water. This accomplished the same goal, but the simpler the heating device is the easier it is going to be able to maintain a steady temperature throughout the cook. Below is the image of the final setup with the power of the water boiler plugged into the female port of the Power Tall 2 and the male port plugged into the power strip. My battery pack that came with my Arduino had died, so I simply used the USB cord and my cell phone charger to power the Arduino.

In order for the meat to cook properly in the water bath, it needs to be in vacuum bag so that all the heat from the water is directly transferred to the meat. I seasoned the steak with a mix of salt, pepper, and a little bit of garlic powder and vacuum sealed the bad and put it in the water bath and left it along for 3 hours.

After the 3 hours of waiting, I took the meet out and finished it on the stove with heated up Olive Oil and butter to get a sear on the outside to add a little bit more flavor. After it was seared to perfection, I obtained a perfectly medium-rare steak that wasn't overcooked.
